On Storytelling

We Are All Storytellers

On the ancient, inescapable human impulse to make meaning through narrative — and why the stories we tell ourselves matter most.

March 2026

Every day, thousands upon thousands of stories are published. Blog posts and newsletters. Short stories and novels. Myths, fables, legends, anecdotes. Feature films, documentaries, multi-season television sagas that consume whole weekends of our lives. When you step back from consuming all of it long enough to take in the sheer scope of what we produce, it might make you wonder: How did we get here? And more pointedly — what exactly are we doing? Are we just distracting ourselves?

I don't think so. The vast, almost incomprehensible volume of stories that humans create and consume points to something far more fundamental than entertainment. It points to the fact that we are social beings who communicate ideas in story-form. We are innate storytellers with a voracious, bone-deep need to make sense of the world by translating experience into narrative.

And we always have been.

The Oldest Technology

Imagine a primitive human hundreds of thousands of years ago who looked at some rainwater that had collected in a shallow depression in the ground. She saw more than a puddle of clear liquid — she noticed what was reflected off the surface, and that it moved. She waved her hand, the light shifted, and she thought: that's me.

It's an unlikely scenario that inadequately tries to explain the origin of self-awareness. But it illustrates a deeper point. At some stage in our evolution, humans developed the capacity to use language — and eventually narrative — to represent ideas about themselves and the world. We started with the observed and the practical. We told stories about where the water was, which berries were poisonous, where the predators slept. And then, as the stories we told transcended pure practicality, we began connecting ideas that don't coexist in observable reality. We imagined things that hadn't happened, things that couldn't happen, and fiction was born.

Homo sapiens has been telling stories for hundreds of thousands of years. Cave paintings, oral traditions, religious parables, epic poems — storytelling has evolved alongside every leap in human cognition. As our mental capacity grew to keep pace with an increasingly complex world, so did our narrative ambitions. We didn't just need to know which way the river ran. We needed to know why we were here at all. And story, more than any other human invention, offered us a vehicle to explore that question.

Neuroscientists at Princeton have even discovered what happens in our brains when stories work. In a landmark 2010 study, researchers found that during effective storytelling, the brain activity of the listener begins to mirror the brain activity of the speaker — a phenomenon called neural coupling. The more closely those patterns aligned, the deeper the listener's comprehension. When someone tells you a story and it lands, your brain is, in a very literal sense, synchronized with theirs. Stories are not just communicated. They are shared at a neurological level. It's the closest thing we have to telepathy.

We Live Inside Stories

Stories help us cope with our lives, yes. But it goes far deeper than simple escapism. I'd argue that our lives and our very identities are built around story. We wake up in the morning and continue the narrative we left off with yesterday, stringing all our days into a coherent arc. That arc runs in the back of our minds and quietly informs most of our thoughts, our decisions, and our sense of who we are.

As the saying goes, we all think of ourselves as the main character in the story of our lives, and everyone around us is a supporting player. But the important thing isn't the cliché. It's the implication: life is a story, one that depends almost entirely on our point of view — and that point of view can shift from moment to moment, year to year, crisis to crisis.

You wake up and go for a run and eat a healthy breakfast because you are living out the story that you are a healthy person. Or you don't, because you believe the story ingrained in your psyche that says you're not that person, that you'll change someday, but not today.

You go to church on Sunday because you grew up inside a story that says you need faith to be whole. Or you don't, because you've constructed a different story in which the empirical world is the only one that matters.

You stay in a relationship because you've told yourself it's too hard to leave. You go to your job because you've told yourself that your current role is a necessary stepping stone in a larger career arc. You believe what you've told yourself about your past, your identity, your capabilities. And you let those elements — your race, your nationality, your gender, your social class, the stories your family handed you — define the role you play on the stage of your life.

Even I'm doing it right now. By writing this essay, I'm trying to live inside the narrative I've formed of myself — that I'm a writer, that I have something worthwhile to say, that I should put these ideas out onto the internet because someone out there will read them and find them valuable. Even if that's not true. Even if no one ever clicks the link. Not every story we tell ourselves comes true.

And that's the thing worth pausing on. Some stories we tell ourselves can trap us. They can limit us, confine us to a version of ourselves that no longer fits — or never did. Imagine waking up every day and telling yourself: I'm not smart enough. I don't deserve this. People like me don't get to do that. You would be imprisoning yourself inside a false narrative about who you are, what you're capable of, and what you deserve.

We all inherit stories from family, from culture, from experience. But they don't always serve us. Sometimes the bravest thing a person can do is rewrite the story they've been living inside.

That, incidentally, is what the greatest novels do. They show us characters who either break free of their false story — or are destroyed by it.

What Makes a Story a Story?

So if storytelling is this fundamental to who we are, it follows that almost anyone can do it. And they can — we don't need permission from anyone to put our stories onto the page. That freedom is exhilarating. It can also be maddening, because translating the murky architecture of our thoughts and feelings into coherent language is one of the hardest things a person can attempt.

Still, most of us develop the ability to tell simple stories even if we never consciously study the mechanics. We understand, instinctively, how to relate an account of connected events with a beginning, middle, and end. It can be real or made up, but it's usually organized around a central conflict and designed to entertain, educate, or reveal something true about being alive.

But there's a difference between telling a story and telling a compelling one. "Life isn't fair" is a story of sorts, but it's not particularly interesting. It's too literal, too flat. A powerful story doesn't just state its theme — it enacts it. It puts you inside an experience rather than handing you a conclusion.

Consider one of the most famous demonstrations of this principle:

"For sale: baby shoes, never worn." — Popularly attributed to Ernest Hemingway, though the true origin is disputed

Six words. No characters named, no scene described, no conflict dramatized in any conventional sense. And yet the story is devastating. It's about tragedy, grief, mortality, unfulfilled dreams — all of it conjured not by what's said, but by what's left unsaid. The author didn't write "losing a child is painful." They represented that idea in a way that makes you feel it, in the hollow of your chest, in the half-second it takes your imagination to fill the silence.

Or consider Augusto Monterroso's famous micro-story, often cited as the shortest piece of fiction ever written:

"When he awoke, the dinosaur was still there." — Augusto Monterroso, from Complete Works (and Other Stories), 1959

Seven words in the original Spanish. Eight in English. And yet it functions as a complete narrative. The word "still" does enormous work — it implies a before, a history we don't see. How long has the dinosaur been there? Has this happened more than once? "Still" suggests persistence across multiple awakenings, though we can't know for how many. There is an unseen past and an unknown future. We're left wondering: what happens now?

And then there's the question of what the dinosaur is. Is it literal? Or does it represent a problem, a fear, a relationship, a grief, an addiction — something that refuses to leave no matter how many times you close your eyes? The story works because it implies that certain things don't go away just because you try to sleep them off. Because you bury your head in the sand. Because you pretend.

The genius of both of these micro-stories is that they give you just enough to activate your imagination — and then they stop. The reader finishes the story by projecting their own experience into the gap. The dinosaur becomes whatever you most need it to be. That's the alchemy of all great fiction: creating a world that is simultaneously specific enough to feel real and open enough to feel personal. Every novel, every film, every myth is really just a longer, more elaborate version of what Monterroso accomplished in a single sentence.

But doing that — writing something that lands with that kind of precision — takes practice. Enormous, deliberate, often frustrating practice. Especially at length.

Why Stories Take the Shape They Do

If you've spent any time studying narrative, you've probably encountered premade story frameworks — the Hero's Journey, the three-act structure, Save the Cat, and dozens of others. These templates can be useful, but their existence raises a more interesting question: why do stories seem to follow recognizable patterns? Who decided that stories need a beginning, a conflict, and a resolution?

Nobody did. The form wasn't invented by ancient Greek theorists or modern screenwriting gurus. It emerged organically, because it maps onto how humans actually experience being alive.

We are born, we live, we die — beginning, middle, end. We want things, we encounter obstacles, we either get what we want or we don't — desire, conflict, resolution. We make decisions that change us, for better or for worse — character arc. The shape of a story is the shape of a human life.

Once you see that, the deeper logic of storytelling starts to come into focus. Stories fulfill specific cognitive and emotional needs that go far beyond entertainment:

The need to make meaning out of chaos. Real life doesn't have a plot. Things happen randomly, without clear cause and effect. Story imposes a shape on that chaos. Beginning, middle, end gives us the sense — maybe the illusion, maybe the truth — that events are connected, that things happen for reasons, that life has direction.
The need to rehearse experience safely. When you read about a character facing loss, betrayal, or an impossible choice, your brain responds almost as if it's happening to you. Neuroscientist Paul Zak's research has shown that emotionally engaging narratives trigger the release of cortisol and oxytocin — the same hormones involved in real stress and real bonding. Story is a flight simulator for the human condition. You can live through catastrophe and heartbreak without any of it touching your actual life.
The need for transformation. We are meaning-seeking creatures who believe, somewhere deep down, that experience should change us. The arc of a character who faces something hard and emerges different mirrors our deepest hope about our own lives — that what we go through means something. That suffering is not random but redemptive.
The need for empathy. Story puts you inside another consciousness. It is, arguably, the only technology humans have ever invented that reliably does this. Reading fiction has been shown to measurably increase empathy, because when you read a character deeply, you are literally practicing the act of being someone else. You're training the muscles of compassion and perspective-taking in a way that no amount of abstract reasoning can replicate.

Story has the form it has because that form is the shape of human need. The two evolved together. We didn't invent narrative structure and then discover it was useful. We needed to make meaning, and narrative structure is what that need looks like when it takes shape in language.

You Have a Story Inside You

Is storytelling an aptitude that some people are born with and others aren't? I think some of us are more naturally inclined toward it, the same way some people are drawn to music or mathematics. But I'd argue that storytelling is also a skill like any other — something you can refine and sharpen through deliberate, sustained practice.

More importantly, I think it's a fundamental human behavior. It's as natural as language itself, perhaps even older. Long before we had words, we had gestures, expressions, and sequences of sounds that conveyed meaning in narrative form. Something happened. Then something else happened. And this is what it meant.

Many people feel the pull to formalize that instinct into actual written work — a novel, a memoir, a poem, an essay like this one. But even those who never write a word are still storytelling, all the time, in the privacy of their own minds. You create stories that live inside your head, shaping your understanding of the past and your expectations for the future, even if it's happening at a level you're barely conscious of.

You've probably heard the old saying that every person has at least one novel inside them. I think that undersells it. I think every person has dozens — whole libraries of untold stories built from the raw material of a life lived, the relationships navigated, the losses absorbed, the small revelations that hit you in the shower or on the drive home or in the middle of the night when sleep won't come.

But there is a difference between the stories we consume and the stories we make. Reading is largely an act of receiving — you enter someone else's world, follow their logic, feel what their characters feel. Writing is something else entirely. It is an act of excavation. You don't fully know what you think about something until you've been forced to put it into words, to find the precise sequence of sentences that captures an experience or an idea with enough accuracy that a stranger might recognize it as true. The page has a way of revealing what you actually believe — as opposed to what you think you believe — and that is a different, harder, more clarifying kind of encounter with yourself than reading ever quite manages to be.

The question isn't whether you have a story. The question is whether you'll let it out.

Not everyone will write a novel. Not everyone should. But I think the world would be a richer, stranger, more compassionate place if more people tried — not because the product matters, but because the process does. Writing a story forces you to do the thing that stories have always done for our species: examine experience, search for meaning, and attempt to communicate something true about what it feels like to be alive.

And that impulse — the one that made our ancestors paint on cave walls, that made a Guatemalan writer distill an entire human predicament into seven Spanish words, that makes you narrate your own life to yourself every morning before your feet hit the floor — that impulse is not a luxury. It's not a distraction. It is one of the most essential things about us.

We are all living inside stories. We always have been. The only question is whether we'll become conscious authors of our own — or remain characters in narratives someone else wrote for us.

I say: pick up the pen.
